# Active Listening and Emotional Bids

## Learning Objectives
- Define Gottman's concept of bids for connection and why they matter.
- Practice active listening skills that communicate curiosity and care.
- Identify common patterns of turning toward, turning away, and turning against.
- Use small responses to build trust and emotional safety.

## Why This Matters
Relationship satisfaction is not built in grand moments. It is shaped by small daily interactions where partners reach for each other emotionally. John Gottman's research on couples highlights that tiny moments of response predict long-term stability. When one partner makes a "bid" for connection, the other partner can respond in ways that deepen trust or erode it. The goal of active listening is not to agree with everything. It is to show that the other person's inner experience is understood and respected.

## What Are Emotional Bids?
A bid is any attempt to connect: a question, a sigh, a shared story, or an invitation to look at something together. The bid can be verbal or nonverbal. Gottman found that couples who stayed together tended to "turn toward" bids by acknowledging them. Couples who struggled tended to ignore or dismiss bids. Turning toward does not require a long conversation. It can be as simple as a nod, a follow-up question, or a brief touch.

## Active Listening Basics
Active listening is a set of behaviors that signal "I am with you." It includes attention, reflection, and validation. Attention means putting distractions aside for a moment to make eye contact or orient your body. Reflection means paraphrasing what you heard, which reduces misunderstandings and signals engagement. Validation means acknowledging the emotion even if you disagree with the point. Validation does not mean approval. It means recognizing that the feeling is real for your partner.

## Common Listening Pitfalls
Many people jump into problem solving, which can feel dismissive if the speaker wants empathy. Another pitfall is "stacking," where you mentally prepare your response instead of listening. A third is minimizing, such as "It is not a big deal." These habits are often unintentional, but they can weaken trust over time. A simple antidote is to slow down and mirror one key idea before you respond. This keeps the conversation grounded in understanding rather than debate.

## Turning Toward, Away, or Against
When a partner makes a bid, you can respond in three broad ways:
- Turning toward: Engaging the bid with interest or care.
- Turning away: Ignoring or missing the bid.
- Turning against: Responding with sarcasm, criticism, or contempt.

Gottman's findings show that turning toward creates a positive feedback loop. It increases the sense that you are on the same team. Turning away is not always malicious; it can be caused by stress, fatigue, or distraction. Turning against is harmful because it communicates rejection and often escalates conflict.

## Micro-Responses Matter
Many people think intimacy requires deep talks. In practice, short responses can be powerful. A quick "Tell me more" or "That makes sense" can transform a moment. These micro-responses are evidence of care. Over time, they accumulate into a felt sense of safety. Gottman calls this the "emotional bank account." Each positive response is a small deposit that makes conflict easier to navigate later.

## Listening for the Emotion Under the Words
People often ask for solutions when they really want empathy. Active listening looks for the emotion beneath the content. For example, "You never call me back" may carry loneliness or fear. A response such as "You felt left out today" will often land better than advice. This approach aligns with evidence-based communication models like Emotionally Focused Therapy, which emphasize attachment needs and emotional attunement.

## Repair in Real Time
No one listens perfectly. What matters is the repair. If you notice you missed a bid or responded sharply, name it: "I think I missed that, can you say it again?" or "I got defensive; I want to understand." Repair attempts are a major predictor of relationship stability in Gottman's research. They show accountability and willingness to reconnect.

## Practice: The 90-Second Pause
Stress can hijack listening. Try a 90-second pause before responding in tense moments. Physiologically, this gives your nervous system time to settle. During the pause, focus on your breath and remind yourself that the goal is connection, not winning. When you respond, lead with curiosity rather than conclusion. A simple "Help me understand" shifts the tone.

## Consent and Listening
Consent conversations are a form of active listening. Asking "Is this okay?" and checking in with body language are bids for safety. Respecting a "no" or a pause is a powerful way to turn toward. It communicates that your partner's comfort is more important than momentum. This builds trust and makes future intimacy feel safer and more mutually desired.

## Takeaway
Active listening is not a technique to win arguments; it is a way to honor your partner's inner world. Gottman, Sue Johnson, and other relationship researchers show that partners who respond to bids build resilience over time. The goal is simple: when your partner reaches out, move toward them with curiosity, care, and respect.

# The Dual Control Model of Arousal

## Learning Objectives
- Describe the dual control model of sexual response.
- Identify common accelerators and brakes for arousal.
- Distinguish between desire and arousal in everyday language.
- Apply the model to improve consent-aware communication.

## A Brief Overview
The dual control model, developed by researchers John Bancroft and Erick Janssen, proposes that sexual response is governed by two systems: excitation (the accelerator) and inhibition (the brakes). Arousal increases when accelerators are active and brakes are quiet. Arousal decreases when brakes are active, even if accelerators are present. This model is supported by research showing that individuals vary in sensitivity to both systems.

## Accelerators and Brakes
Accelerators are cues that increase interest or arousal. They can be physical (touch, warmth), contextual (privacy, time), relational (feeling valued), or psychological (fantasy, novelty). Brakes are cues that decrease arousal or make it unsafe. Common brakes include stress, fatigue, conflict, fear of judgment, lack of privacy, or past experiences that make a situation feel risky.

A key insight is that brakes are often stronger than accelerators. This is adaptive for safety. If a person feels unsafe or pressured, the brakes can override the accelerator even if they care deeply about their partner.

## Desire vs. Arousal
Desire is the motivation or interest in connection. Arousal is the physiological and psychological readiness that follows. People sometimes experience desire without arousal or arousal without desire. The model helps couples avoid personalizing these differences. It also emphasizes that arousal is influenced by context, not only by attraction.

## Individual Differences
People differ in how sensitive their accelerators and brakes are. Some people have a "fast accelerator" and a "slow brake," which means they respond quickly to cues and are less affected by inhibitors. Others have a "slow accelerator" and a "fast brake," which means they need more conducive conditions and are easily shut down by stress. Neither pattern is better. Understanding each partner's wiring reduces blame and increases empathy.

## Mapping Your Brakes and Accelerators
Try a simple mapping exercise: write down five accelerators and five brakes from your recent experiences. Be specific about context. An accelerator might be "a long hug after dinner" while a brake might be "worrying about a deadline." This exercise is not a scorecard; it is a shared language for understanding. Couples who can name their brakes find it easier to reduce them without guessing.

## Why Context Matters
Emily Nagoski, in her synthesis of sexual research, emphasizes that context is the engine of response. If the environment is chaotic or judgmental, brakes engage. If the environment is relaxed, supportive, and consenting, brakes ease and accelerators can work. This explains why desire can feel inconsistent. It is often a reflection of the context, not a reflection of love.

## Consent and the Brakes
Consent is a primary brake-release mechanism. When a person feels they can say "no" without consequences, the brakes soften. Pressure, guilt, or coercion trigger the brakes. Clear, enthusiastic consent tells the nervous system that it is safe to respond. This is why consent is not a formality; it is the foundation of relaxed arousal.

## Long-Term Relationships and Habituation
In long-term partnerships, familiarity can dampen accelerators. This does not mean desire is gone; it means novelty may require intentionality. Small changes to routine, shared curiosity, and emotional attunement can add gentle accelerators without pressure. The model encourages couples to focus on context rather than searching for a single fix.

## A Practical Exercise
List three accelerators and three brakes for yourself. Then share them with your partner, if appropriate. This conversation is not about demand. It is about understanding. For example: "An accelerator for me is uninterrupted time. A brake is feeling rushed." This invites collaboration around creating context.

## A Short Dialogue Script
If this topic feels sensitive, try a gentle script: "I have been learning about accelerators and brakes. I want to understand mine and yours better. Would you be open to sharing one thing that helps you feel relaxed and one thing that shuts you down?" Keeping it small and specific makes the conversation safer and more productive.

## The "Good Enough" Model
The dual control model also challenges perfectionism. You do not need a perfect scenario. You need enough accelerators and fewer brakes. Focus on reducing one key brake rather than adding ten accelerators. This is a realistic and compassionate way to build intimacy.

## Takeaway
Bancroft and Janssen's dual control model explains why arousal is dynamic and context-sensitive. When partners learn their accelerators and brakes, they can design interactions that prioritize safety, consent, and connection. The model replaces blame with curiosity and turns desire into a shared exploration rather than a test. Over time, this shared language can reduce pressure and increase mutual understanding.
